Age-Progression Research App

Department of Engineering & Public Policy, CMU

Oct.2017 - Sept.2018

My Role & Project Goal

I designed a desktop app that allows the researchers to create 3D content for their research experiment, keeping in mind that it's for non-technical users. It needed to build age-progression 3D personalized assets for eventual use in a VR environment built with Unity. I worked with a programmer, and a project manager.

Clinical Trials

This research app is for use in hospital clinical trials. In the trial, patients will see themselves age in VR.

Process - Phase 1

Building A Viable Deployment Pipeline

The client required the FaceGen software developed by Singular Inversions to generate facial age progression 3D model based on other successful studies.
Reference: Increasing Saving Behavior Through Age-Progressed Renderings of the Future Self --by Hal E.Hershfield


"Our early mission was to incorporate the output of FaceGen with Oculus Go VR headset."


What FaceGen can do:

Generate a parameter-driven 3D head model with one frontal, and 2 side (left, right) photos.

What FaceGen can't do:

Generate specific characteristics that help personalize the 3D representation of the user’s face (e.g. hairstyles, facial features etc)

I needed to create a app load the 3D head model from FaceGen, attach external hairstyles, then push the final model to the Oculus Go.

Phase 2

Building The App Core Functions

The 3 core functions of this app are:

User Interface Layout

Load the head model & Rotate

Select hairstyles

Change hair colors & grey hair level

​Design Iterations

I iterated the design based on researchers' feedback through the usability tests.

Looking at hairstyle creation:

Plans to create hairstyles:

I decided to generate hair by making a variety of 3D mesh hairstyles, due to processing performance, good fidelity, and reasonable development time.

I created 9 short hairstyles, 7 long hairstyles in Maya, and itergrated with the app by optimizing the textures, and transformational data.

Phase 3

Final Result In Oculus Go

Our client wanted a simple VR experience that simulates looking into a mirror with the natural scape background.

Interaction: The mirror will follow along with the head movement.

Other interaction workarounds with controllers

I created and pitched these VR interaction mockups to the client to show how a 3D head could be displayed in the Oculus Go. In the end, the client wanted a simple option that wouldn’t need any controllers. This was because they wanted to eliminate any friction in the patient on boarding that might distract from the core of their clinical trial process.

Lesson Learned

Design perspective:

  • Creating workarounds in the design when our team ran into technical hurdles.

    • Making a modular framework for installing different hairstyles on the models.


  • Understanding the context of the research trial and updating the design accordingly.

    • The researchers needed an approximation of facial features, and quickly generating models was more valuable for them vs. photorealistic mockups. As a result, I slimmed down the options to emphasize ease and quickness for the researchers to focus more of their attention on participant interaction.

Client relationship perspective:

  • Working hard in the beginning of the project to define scope and accurately determine time commitment needed to ensure staying under budget.

  • Meeting frequently with the client to update the pipeline and design. This meant shorter sprints to allow the client to have more input in the design features.
